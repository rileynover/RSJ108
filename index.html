How to determine what medium is best for your story

Assembly time: 5 minutes
Awesome rating: 4% awesome

How can you best tell a story? It might work great as just an article, maybe it's best suited to an audio-only radio or podcast format, or perhaps it's a visually rich story that requires video.

  • Things You'll Need

  • Computer
  • Camera
  • Microphone
  • Human Brain
  • Journalistic Ethics
  • Water(for drinking)

  1. Determine the visual appeal of your story.

    Is your story easier to understand when you can see what is happening? Does a visual image inspire emotions? Can your audience better relate to the subjects of the story visually? Does your story just look cool? If so, a medium with visuals may work best, either an article with frequent images or a video story.

  2. How informationally dense is your story?

    If your story has a lot of important, quantitative information that you need to get across in a brief period of time, an article may work best. For less informational, more human-focused stories, a podcast or video story might be the option where the audience can see and hear the subjects. If a quantitative information-heavy story is difficult to understand at a glance, a video story with data visualizations might be helpful.

    Nature-focused stories often benefit from visual elements. Pictured is a goose relaxing at Manzanita Lake in March 2024. Photo by Riley Overstreet.

  3. What is the content of your story?

    A story focused on a single interview may be best suited to a podcast, so that the audience can listen and feel like they're there in the interview in real time. Broader stories that take you to different places and feature multiple interviews are better suited to video or article formats.

  4. What equipment is viable to bring to the site of the story?

    Depending on the story and your circumstance, your equipment may determine the type of story you produce. If you're there when a story happens with nothing but your phone, it may be best to compile audio interviews and take photos to later turn into an article. Camera equipment is the next best choice, as depending on what you're using a camera and microphone can be relatively small and transportable; you can travel to the story. The most difficult option is a podcast, as high quality audio equipment is nowhere near as intuitive to use on the fly as the other choices. You often have to have guests come to a studio to record, or compile information like an article and recite it in podcast format.

  5. What does your editor say?

    Perhaps the most important step, and the one least out of your control, is to listen to your editor on the matter. They probably know better than you do.
